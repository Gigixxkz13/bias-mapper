Bias Mapper: A Tool for Visualizing and Evaluating AI Worldviews

Bias Mapper

A dark educational interface for exploring how small shifts in identity and framing can alter the responses of large language models and create measurable informational bubbles.

✦ ✦ ✦

What is Bias Mapper?

Bias Mapper is a web-based research tool designed to reveal how small changes in prompts can shape AI-generated responses in measurably different ways. Users are increasingly turning to systems like ChatGPT and Copilot for information gathering — yet these tools are trained on large-scale collections of human-written data, meaning they can unintentionally inherit, reproduce, or even intensify human cognitive biases.

By comparing paired prompts that differ by only one variable — such as the gender, age, or location of the implied user — the system makes hidden patterns visible and measurable through structured analysis, helping users understand how AI can produce different "worldviews" depending on how a prompt is framed.

Bias Mapper does not attempt to remove bias. Its purpose is to make it visible, structured, and easier to observe through direct comparison.

Theoretical Background

Large Language Models are trained on vast corpora of human-generated text, meaning they automatically absorb the cognitive biases embedded in that data. The following bias types form the theoretical foundation of this research.

01

Anchoring Bias

When making decisions, an initial piece of information gets relied on excessively — even when that information is unsupported or logically irrelevant. In LLMs, even slight changes in phrasing, such as including extra context or implied perspectives, can serve as anchoring guides that narrow the model's interpretive space, causing two logically equivalent prompts to produce meaningfully different outputs.

02

Confirmation Bias

AI systems reproduce confirmation bias by generating responses that support the framing introduced by users. When a particular conclusion is implied in a prompt, that assumption is usually reinforced by the model rather than questioned. In interactive settings, users may further reinforce this bias by accepting agreeable outputs and dismissing conflicting ones, creating feedback loops that narrow interpretations over time.

03

Primacy Bias

LLMs exhibit measurable sensitivity to early information within a prompt. Empirical research shows that the order of input markedly influences model output — when the first part of a prompt sets a direction, the model's reasoning often remains confined to that path even when later elements introduce corrections. This positional preference persists even when semantic content remains unchanged, indicating the effect arises from prompt structure rather than meaning.

04

Framing Effect

Different conclusions are drawn based on how information is presented, particularly when framed positively or negatively. Since LLMs generate responses from human-authored text, even slight changes in phrasing and labelling can channel responses toward distinct interpretive paths. Framing plays a foundational role in how LLMs produce outputs — exerting a broader and more persistent influence than explicit content alone.

05

Representativeness Bias

LLMs learn which language patterns, viewpoints, and associations appear most frequently in training data, and generate responses that align with these familiar templates. Less common or non-prototypical perspectives are systematically pushed aside. Over time, repeated reliance on representativeness compresses distinct identities and contexts into simplistic statistical profiles — operating silently, making resulting distortions difficult to detect.

06

Bias Inheritance & Feedback Loops

Bias in LLMs does not remain static after introduction. As model outputs are reused, cited, and reformulated as future inputs, earlier patterns quietly re-enter the system and are reinforced. Through self-improvement mechanisms such as Chain-of-Thought prompting and self-consistency, biased interpretations that survive self-selection gain legitimacy through repetition — becoming increasingly fluent, consistent, and difficult to detect.

07

Information Bubbles & Algorithmic Filtering

AI systems are no longer neutral tools — they stand between the user and available information, deciding what content is prioritized, ranked, and filtered based on inferred preferences and past interactions. Consistent exposure to similar content produces information bubbles: informational spaces in which perspectives are systematically reinforced while unique viewpoints are underrepresented. These bubbles do not result from intentional manipulation, but from the interaction between personalization mechanisms, model optimization objectives, and human behaviour. Bias Mapper makes these bubbles visible by measuring how prompt variations produce distinct informational outputs from the same model.

Bubble Types Explored

Six controlled prompt pairs form the experimental core of this research. Each pair isolates a single variable to test whether the model produces measurably different outputs based on that attribute alone.

I

Gender-Based Bubble

Tests whether career recommendations, framing, or thematic emphasis shift depending on the implied gender of the user. Examines whether the model reproduces gender stereotypes embedded in its training data when the only difference between two prompts is the stated gender identity.

II

Geographic Bubble

Explores how location-based identity affects AI output by comparing prompts that differ only in the user's stated region or nationality. Measures whether the model disproportionately emphasises certain cultural contexts or resources based on geographic framing alone.

III

Socioeconomic Bubble

Examines whether income-based framing changes the nature of recommendations. Compares prompts attributed to a high-income versus a low-income user to test whether the model adjusts its suggestions in ways that reflect or reinforce socioeconomic assumptions from its training data.

IV

Age-Based Bubble

Compares outputs generated for younger and older implied users on the same topic. Investigates whether the model makes age-based assumptions about capability, preference, or relevance — and whether these assumptions are consistent with or diverge from the factual content of the request.

V

Neutral vs Positive Framing

Measures how positively charged language in a prompt influences tone, emphasis, and content in the model's response. Tests whether the model amplifies the positive framing introduced by the user rather than maintaining balance — a direct instance of the framing effect in generative AI.

VI

Neutral vs Emotional Framing

Tests whether embedding emotional language in a prompt shifts the vocabulary, sentiment, and thematic emphasis of the model's output. Examines whether the model mirrors or amplifies the emotional register of the prompt, reflecting confirmation bias dynamics in which the model reinforces the affective framing introduced by the user.

How the System Works

1

Select a predefined controlled prompt pair or build a guided one by choosing from structured attribute options.

2

Send Prompt A and Prompt B to the selected AI model — OpenAI GPT-4o-mini or Anthropic Claude Haiku.

3

Store both responses in the database and process them through the analysis engine.

4

Measure sentiment polarity, category diversity, and thematic keyword emphasis across both responses.

5

Visualize the measurable "bubble effect" through side-by-side charts, showing how prompt variations produce distinct informational outputs from the same model.

Controlled Experimental Mode
